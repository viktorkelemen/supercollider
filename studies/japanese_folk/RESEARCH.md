## Historical Overview

### Origins

Japanese folk music (min'yo, 民謡) traces its origins to ancient times, evolving through the Jomon, Yayoi, and subsequent historical periods. The earliest forms were linked to agricultural rituals and Shinto ceremonies, with influences from Chinese and Korean music arriving via the Silk Road.

- **Min'yo** (民謡): Compound of "folk/people" (民, min) and "song" (謡, yo)
- First recorded usage in Japan: **901 AD**
- Modern scholarly usage emerged after the **Meiji Restoration** (1868) as a calque of the German "Volkslied"

### The Court Tradition: Gagaku

**Gagaku** (雅楽, "elegant music") represents Japan's oldest classical music tradition:

- Introduced from Tang China via Korean Peninsula in the **7th-9th centuries**
- Completed in artistic form by the **10th century**
- Inscribed on **UNESCO's Intangible Cultural Heritage** list in 2009
- Still performed at the Imperial Palace for state functions

### Edo Period (1603-1868)

The Edo period saw the flourishing of folk traditions alongside urban entertainment music:

- **Shamisen** became central to both folk and theatrical music
- Regional folk songs crystallized into distinct styles
- Festival music (matsuri-bayashi) developed elaborate forms

### Modern Preservation

- **Machida Kasho** began systematic recording and transcription of regional min'yo in the 1920s
- **Esashi Oiwake National Contest** established in 1963 for vocal performance
- Folk song contests (min'yo taikai) continue to draw thousands of participants
- Contemporary artists blend min'yo with electronic and world music

## Musical Styles

### 1. Min'yo (Folk Song)

**Origin**: Orally transmitted songs from rural communities

**Categories by Function**:
- **Taue uta** (田植え唄): Rice planting songs
- **Mugiuchi uta** (麦打ち唄): Barley threshing songs
- **Amihiki uta** (網引き唄): Net-pulling songs for fishing
- **Sato uta**: Village songs for entertainment

**Performance Types**:
- **Ondo** (音頭): Songs with distinctive swing rhythm, commonly heard at Obon festivals
- **Bushi** (節): Songs with distinctive melodies (e.g., "Tsugaru Jongara-bushi")

**Characteristics**:
- Pentatonic scales (primarily yo-scale on mainland)
- **Kobushi** (こぶし): Characteristic throat vibrato ornament
- Simple, repetitive structures emphasizing communal expression
- Originally sung unaccompanied or heterophonically

---

### 2. Gagaku (Court Music)

**Origin**: Imperial court tradition from the 8th century

**Categories**:
- **Kangen** (管弦): Purely instrumental music
- **Bugaku** (舞楽): Dance with music
- **Saibara**: Folk-influenced court songs
- **Ritual music**: Shinto shrine ceremonies

**Sub-categories by Origin**:
- **Togaku** (唐楽): "Music of the Left" - Chinese Tang dynasty and Indian influences
- **Komagaku** (高麗楽): "Music of the Right" - Korean and other Asian influences

**Characteristics**:
- Heterophonic texture: wind instruments (sho, hichiriki, ryuteki) play the same melody with individual embellishments and slight timing variations
- Extremely slow tempo (single phrases can last 30+ seconds)
- Fixed repertoire unchanged for ~1,400 years
- Free-flowing rhythm following breath patterns
- Sho provides sustained chord clusters underneath the melody

---

### 3. Shomyo (声明) - Buddhist Chant

**Origin**: Introduced with Buddhism from China and Korea, 6th-8th centuries

**Significance**: One of the oldest continuous vocal traditions in Japan, major influence on secular music development.

**Characteristics**:
- **Monophonic**: Single melodic line, no harmony
- **Melismatic**: Extended melodic passages on single syllables
- Uses both Yo and In scale patterns
- Free rhythm following text prosody
- Call-and-response between solo priest and chorus

**Influence on Folk Music**:
- Melodic contours of min'yo often reflect shomyo patterns
- Descending phrases common to both traditions
- Breath-based phrasing originates here
- Kobushi ornamentation has roots in Buddhist melisma

**Styles**:
- **Tendai shomyo**: More elaborate, melodic
- **Shingon shomyo**: Mystical, esoteric character

---

### 4. Okinawan/Ryukyuan Music

**Origin**: Distinct tradition from the Ryukyu Kingdom (15th-19th centuries)

**Categories**:
- **Ryukyu koten ongaku** (琉球古典音楽): Classical chamber music
- **Uta-sanshin** (歌三線): Singing while playing sanshin
- **Folk songs**: Village and festival music

**Characteristics**:
- **Ryukyu scale**: C-E-F-G-B (distinctly different from mainland scales)
- Abundant half-steps (between E-F and B-C)
- Sanshin (three-stringed lute) as defining instrument
- Strong influence on modern Okinawan pop ("Uchinaa pop")

---

### 5. Bon Odori (Obon Dance Music)

**Origin**: Buddhist festival dance tradition (~600 years old)

**Function**: Originally Nenbutsu folk dance to welcome spirits of the dead

**Characteristics**:
- Regional variations in dance, costume, and music
- Simple, repetitive melodies for communal dancing
- Often features taiko drums prominently
- Each region has distinctive songs and choreography

**Notable Regional Styles**:
- **Awa Odori** (Tokushima): Famous energetic dance
- **Gujo Odori** (Gifu): All-night dancing tradition
- **Tanko Bushi** (Fukuoka): Coal-mining dance

---

### 6. Enka (Modern Ballad)

**Origin**: Post-war era, evolved from min'yo traditions

**Characteristics**:
- Pentatonic scales from folk tradition
- **Kobushi** vocal technique central to the style
- Deep vibrato (yuri)
- Themes of love, loss, and longing
- Orchestral arrangements with traditional inflections

**Distinction from Pure Folk**:
- Composed songs (not traditional)
- Professional solo performance
- Urban, not rural context

---

### 7. Theatrical Music (Noh & Kabuki)

While not strictly folk music, theatrical traditions share instruments and techniques with min'yo.

**Noh Music (能楽)**:
- **Hayashi** ensemble: fue (flute), ko-tsuzumi, o-tsuzumi, taiko
- **Utai**: Vocal chanting by actors
- Sparse, ritualistic texture
- Kakegoe calls coordinate ensemble ("Yo!", "Ho!", "Iya!")
- Extremely slow, meditative pacing

**Kabuki Music (歌舞伎)**:
- **Nagauta**: Shamisen-led ensemble music
- **Geza**: Offstage sound effects and atmosphere
- More dynamic and theatrical than Noh
- Uses full range of traditional instruments

**Relevance to Folk**:
- Same instruments (shamisen, fue, tsuzumi)
- Kakegoe technique shared with taiko/festival music
- Ma and jo-ha-kyu principles apply
- Melodic vocabulary overlaps with min'yo

## Regional Traditions

### Tsugaru Region (Aomori Prefecture)

- **Tsugaru-jamisen**: Virtuosic shamisen style
- Characterized by fast tempo and improvisation
- **Tsugaru Jongara-bushi**: Famous showpiece for shamisen

### Tohoku Region (Northern Honshu)

- Rich tradition of agricultural and fishing songs
- **Saitaro-bushi** (Miyagi): Songs of great fish catches
- **Aizu-Bandaisan** (Fukushima): Volcano and harvest songs
- **Sohma Bon Uta**: One of most popular Obon songs

### Hokkaido

- **Soran Bushi**: Famous herring fishermen's song
- Ainu music (typically classified separately from min'yo)

### Kanto Region (Tokyo Area)

- **Tokyo Ondo**: Commissioned in 1932 for department store Bon Odori
- Urban festival songs

### Kansai Region (Osaka/Kyoto)

- Lively festival songs
- Strong shamisen tradition
- **Gion Bayashi**: Festival music for Gion Matsuri

### Niigata / Echigo Region

- One of Japan's most important min'yo regions
- Host of major national folk song competitions
- **Sado Okesa**: Famous song from Sado Island, known for graceful dance
- **Echigo-jishi**: Lion dance tradition
- Strong connection to rice cultivation and fishing

### Amami Islands

Culturally and musically between mainland Japan and Okinawa:
- **Shima-uta** (島唄): Island songs with distinctive high-pitched vocal style
- Uses scales related to both Ryukyu and mainland traditions
- **Sanshin** used (Okinawan influence) but tuning differs
- Famous singer: **Ikue Asazaki**
- More melismatic and emotionally intense than mainland min'yo

### Okinawa and Ryukyu Islands

- Distinct musical tradition (see Okinawan Music above)
- Different scales, language, and instruments
- **Sanshin** rather than shamisen
- Songs like "Tinsagu nu Hana" and "Asadoya Yunta"

## Scales and Modes

### Overview

Japanese music uses several pentatonic scales. Unlike Western music theory, the root note is traditionally considered to be the **central third note**, reflecting concepts of balance.

The five notes symbolically represent the five elements: earth, water, fire, wood, and metal.

---

### 1. Yo Scale (陽旋法) - "Bright Scale"

**Also Called**: Major pentatonic, folk scale

**Structure**: Whole, Minor 3rd, Whole, Whole, Minor 3rd

**Intervals from Root**: 1 - 2 - 4 - 5 - 6 (no 3 or 7)

**Semitones**: 2 - 3 - 2 - 2 - 3

```
Example in D: D - E - G - A - B
```

**Character**: Bright, cheerful, open

**Usage**:
- Folk songs from main islands
- Early popular songs
- Gagaku and shomyo (Buddhist chant)

---

### 2. In Scale (陰旋法) - "Dark Scale"

**Also Called**: Minor pentatonic, urban scale

**Structure**: Contains semitones, creating darker mood

**Intervals from Root**: 1 - b2 - 4 - 5 - b6

**Semitones**: 1 - 4 - 2 - 1 - 4

```
Example in A: A - Bb - D - E - F
```

**Character**: Dark, melancholic, mysterious

**Usage**:
- Urban music
- Shamisen pieces
- Creates the "Japanese sound" recognizable in Western music

---

### 3. Hirajoshi Scale (平調子)

**Origin**: Koto tuning adapted from shamisen by Yatsuhashi Kengyo

**Intervals from Root**: 1 - 2 - b3 - 5 - b6

**Semitones**: 2 - 1 - 4 - 1 - 4

```
Example in C: C - D - Eb - G - Ab
```

**Character**: Contemplative, elegant, melancholic

**Usage**:
- Traditional koto music
- "Sakura" is in this mode

---

### 4. Kumoijoshi Scale (雲井調子)

**Origin**: Another traditional koto tuning

**Intervals from Root**: 1 - b2 - 4 - 5 - b6

**Semitones**: 1 - 4 - 2 - 1 - 4

```
Example in D: D - Eb - G - A - Bb
```

**Character**: Haunting, ethereal

**Note**: Sometimes considered same as In scale in different mode

---

### 5. Iwato Scale (岩戸)

**Origin**: Named after the cave where sun goddess Amaterasu hid

**Intervals from Root**: 1 - b2 - 4 - b5 - b7

**Semitones**: 1 - 4 - 1 - 4 - 2

```
Example in C: C - Db - F - Gb - Bb
```

**Character**: Dark, mysterious, ancient

**Usage**: Evokes Shinto mythology

---

### 6. Ryukyu Scale (琉球音階)

**Distinct from Mainland Scales**

**Intervals from Root**: 1 - 3 - 4 - 5 - 7

**Semitones**: 4 - 1 - 2 - 4 - 1

```
Example in C: C - E - F - G - B
```

**Character**: Distinctive "Okinawan" sound

**Note**: Has semitones between 3-4 and 7-1, giving unique color

---

### Modal Relationships

The Japanese pentatonic scales are modes of each other:

| Mode | Starting Degree | Character |
|------|----------------|-----------|
| Hirajoshi | 1st | Foundational koto mode |
| Iwato | 2nd | Dark, cave-like |
| Kumoi | 3rd | Ethereal |
| Hon Kumoi Shiouzhi | 4th | Variant |
| 5th mode | 5th | Related to In scale |

## Rhythmic Concepts

### Ma (間) - The Space Between

The most distinctive rhythmic concept in Japanese music is **ma** (間), meaning "space," "gap," or "pause."

**Philosophical Foundation**:
- Silence is not absence but presence
- "Good ma" and "bad ma" refer to quality of proportions between sound and silence
- Takemitsu: "The unsounded part... has a deep, powerful, and rich resonance"

**Practical Implications**:
- No steady beat in many traditional pieces
- Solo shakuhachi (honkyoku) has no bar lines or time signatures
- Phrase lengths determined by breath
- Rhythms "flow, attempting to reflect the feeling of nature"

---

### Jo-Ha-Kyu (序破急)

A tripartite temporal aesthetic governing all Japanese arts:

- **Jo** (序): Introduction - slow, spacious beginning
- **Ha** (破): Development - breaking, acceleration
- **Kyu** (急): Conclusion - rapid, intense ending

**Application**:
- Governs single notes to entire compositions
- Regulates tempo changes within phrases
- Creates organic flow between sections

---

### Common Metric Patterns

When regular rhythm is present:

| Pattern | Context |
|---------|---------|
| Free rhythm | Shakuhachi honkyoku, gagaku |
| 2/4 swing | Ondo (festival songs) |
| 4/4 | Many min'yo |
| Breath-based | All traditional music |

**Note**: "In Japanese music, one cannot beat time with one's hands because there is an interval called ma."

## Melodic Structure

### Phrase Construction

- Phrases tied to breath (one breath = one phrase)
- Flexible phrase lengths
- Melodic motion emphasizes small intervals and ornaments
- Descending phrases common

### Ornamentation (Kobushi)

**Kobushi (小節)**: The signature vocal ornament

**Technical Description**:
- Melismatic embellishment on single syllable
- Pitch fluctuates ~70 Hz within one scale degree
- Can occur in as little as 30 milliseconds
- Qualitatively different from Western vibrato

**Types**:
- **Ko-bushi**: Resembles stylized crying, heightens emotional impact
- **Yuri** (揺り): Wavering pitch more like Western vibrato

---

### Common Melodic Patterns

| Pattern | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Descending lines | Characteristic of older songs |
| Small intervals | Steps and minor thirds predominate |
| Repeated notes | Common in work songs |
| Octave displacement | For dramatic effect |
| Cadential figures | Often descend to tonic |

---

### Heterophony (異音性)

The defining texture of Japanese ensemble music. Unlike Western harmony (vertical chords) or counterpoint (independent horizontal lines), heterophony is *diagonal* - multiple instruments play the **same melody** but with individual timing variations and ornaments.

**Characteristics**:
- Creates a "blurred unison" effect
- Each performer adds their own ornaments
- Notes arrive at slightly different times
- No fixed vertical alignment

**Where It Appears**:
- **Sankyoku**: Koto, shamisen, and shakuhachi all play the melody with personal variations
- **Gagaku**: Wind instruments layer the same tune with individual embellishments
- **Min'yo**: Multiple shamisens accompany a singer heterophonically

**Aesthetic Purpose**:
- Reflects the Japanese value of individual expression within group harmony
- Creates shimmering, organic texture
- Avoids the "locked" feel of Western unison

**Implementation Note**: To synthesize heterophony, delay and ornament variations of the same melody across multiple voices (see Implementation Notes section for code).

## Traditional Instruments

### String Instruments

#### Koto (琴)

**Description**: 13-string zither, ~2 meters long, made of Paulownia wood

**Playing Technique**:
- Plucked with picks (tsume) on thumb and first two fingers
- Left hand presses/pulls strings to bend pitch
- Movable bridges (kotoji) for tuning

**Role**: Solo instrument; ensemble with shamisen and shakuhachi (sankyoku)

**Sound Character**:
- Bright, crystalline attack
- Can produce pitch bends and vibratos
- Spectral centroid: ~1500-4000 Hz

---

#### Shamisen (三味線)

**Description**: Three-stringed lute, 1.1-1.4 meters long

**Construction**:
- Body covered in cat/dog skin (or synthetic)
- Strings of twisted silk (different thicknesses)
- Large plectrum (bachi) of tortoiseshell or ivory

**Shamisen Types** (by neck thickness):
- **Hosozao** (細棹, thin neck): Nagauta, jiuta - refined, delicate playing
- **Chuuzao** (中棹, medium neck): Min'yo, folk music - balanced tone
- **Futozao** (太棹, thick neck): Tsugaru-jamisen, gidayu - powerful, percussive

**Playing Techniques**:
- **Tataki**: Striking strings percussively
- Mid-performance tuning adjustments
- Dramatic string bends
- **Hajiki**: Plucking with left hand fingers
- **Sukui**: Upstroke with plectrum

**Sawari (さわり) - The Buzz**:
A defining timbral characteristic of shamisen:
- The lowest string (ichi-no-ito) is set to lightly touch a special groove in the nut
- Creates a controlled buzzing/rattling when the string vibrates
- Adds sustain, brightness, and the characteristic "fuzzy" attack
- Similar in concept to sitar sympathetic resonance
- Essential for authentic shamisen sound synthesis

**Regional Styles**:
- **Tsugaru-jamisen**: Fast, virtuosic, improvisatory (Aomori)
- **Nagauta**: Kabuki theater accompaniment
- **Jiuta**: Chamber music tradition

**Sound Character**:
- Percussive attack with sustain
- Buzzing sawari tone quality
- Spectral centroid: ~1000-3000 Hz

---

#### Sanshin (三線)

**Description**: Okinawan three-stringed lute, precursor to shamisen

**Construction**:
- Body covered in snakeskin
- Plucked with plectrum on index finger

**Role**: Central to all Okinawan music traditions

**Sound Character**: Brighter and mellower than shamisen

---

#### Biwa (琵琶)

**Description**: Short-necked lute, introduced from Tang China (7th century)

**Types**:
- **Gaku-biwa**: Used in gagaku, 4 strings, played with small plectrum
- **Heike-biwa**: For epic storytelling (Tale of Heike), 4 strings
- **Satsuma-biwa**: Larger, more percussive, 4-5 strings, dramatic narratives
- **Chikuzen-biwa**: Softer tone, lyrical pieces, 4-5 strings

**Playing Characteristics**:
- Large wooden plectrum (bachi) strikes strings percussively
- Sawari-like buzz on certain frets
- Dramatic sweeping strokes across all strings
- Pitch bends by pressing behind the fret

**Usage**:
- Narrative storytelling (Heike Biwa - blind monk tradition)
- Gagaku ensemble (gaku-biwa)
- Buddhist ceremonies
- Solo dramatic recitation

**Sound Character**:
- Percussive attack with resonant sustain
- Buzzing overtones from sawari
- Dark, dramatic timbre
- Spectral centroid: ~800-2000 Hz

---

### Wind Instruments

#### Shakuhachi (尺八)

**Description**: Vertical bamboo flute, ~54cm (1 shaku 8 sun) standard length

**Construction**:
- Root-end of bamboo used
- 5 finger holes (4 front, 1 back)
- Carved utaguchi (mouthpiece)

**Playing Technique**:
- Sound produced by blowing across edge (no reed)
- Extensive pitch bending through lip and finger adjustment
- Breath-based phrasing

**Meri/Kari (Pitch Bending)**:
The two fundamental techniques for pitch alteration:
- **Meri** (メリ): Lower pitch by tilting chin down toward chest, partially shading finger holes. Can lower pitch by a semitone or more. Creates darker, more introspective tone.
- **Kari** (カリ): Raise pitch by tilting chin up, opening embouchure. Creates brighter, more projecting tone.
- These techniques allow the 5-hole instrument to play chromatically
- Essential for expressive playing and achieving notes between the natural fingerings

**Other Techniques**:
- **Muraiki**: Breathy, diffuse attack
- **Tamane**: Rapid tongue flutter
- **Yuri**: Vibrato created by jaw movement

**Repertoire**:
- **Honkyoku**: Solo meditative pieces (Fuke sect)
- **Sankyoku**: Ensemble with koto and shamisen

**Sound Character**:
- Breathy, ethereal tone
- Wide pitch and dynamic flexibility
- Spectral centroid: ~800-2000 Hz

---

#### Shinobue (篠笛)

**Description**: Transverse bamboo flute

**Usage**:
- Festival music (matsuri-bayashi)
- Min'yo accompaniment
- Kabuki theater

**Sound Character**: Bright, piercing

---

#### Sho (笙)

**Description**: Mouth organ with 17 bamboo pipes

**Unique Feature**:
- Only Japanese instrument capable of true chords
- Sounds on both inhale and exhale

**Usage**: Gagaku ensemble

**Sound Character**: Soft, glowing, continuous harmonies

---

#### Hichiriki (篳篥)

**Description**: Small double-reed instrument

**Usage**: Carries melody in gagaku

**Sound Character**: Reedy, intense, nasal

---

### Percussion

#### Taiko (太鼓)

**Types**:
- **Miya-daiko**: Long-bodied barrel drums
- **Oke-daiko**: Bucket-shaped drums
- **Shime-daiko**: Small, high-pitched drums

**Playing Technique**:
- Struck with wooden sticks (bachi)
- Multiple players often on one large drum
- Variations in pitch/tone through striking location
- Center hits = deeper, fuller tone
- Rim hits = higher, sharper crack

**Kakegoe (掛け声) - Vocal Calls**:
Essential rhythmic and energetic element of taiko and ensemble playing:
- **"Iya!"** or **"Yoi!"**: Call to attention, signals coming phrase
- **"Ha!"**: Accent, punctuation
- **"Sore!"**: Encouragement, similar to "go!"
- **"Yo!"** or **"Ho!"**: Rhythmic filler calls
- Creates energy, coordinates ensemble, fills rhythmic gaps
- Also used in other ensemble contexts (matsuri, bon odori)

**Role**: Festival music, ensemble, theatrical

---

#### Tsuzumi (鼓)

**Description**: Hour-glass shaped hand drum

**Types**:
- **Ko-tsuzumi**: Small, held at shoulder
- **O-tsuzumi**: Larger, held at hip

**Usage**: Noh and Kabuki theater

```supercollider
    var env = EnvGen.kr(Env(
        [0, depth, depth.neg * 0.7, depth * 0.3, 0],  // rise, fall, partial recovery
        [0.02, 0.04, 0.03, 0.03],
        \sine
    ));
    baseFreq * (1 + env)
};

// Kobushi that triggers on note attacks
~kobushiTrig = { |baseFreq, trig, depth = 30|  // depth in Hz, ~70Hz is authentic
    var env = EnvGen.kr(
        Env([0, 1, -0.6, 0.2, 0], [0.015, 0.025, 0.02, 0.02]),
        trig
    );
    baseFreq + (env * depth)
};
```

```supercollider
// Meri - downward pitch bend (chin down, darker tone)
~meri = { |baseFreq, depth = 0.5|  // depth in semitones

    baseFreq * (2 ** (bend / 12))
};

};

// Expressive meri/kari phrase ending (common cadential gesture)
~meriKariCadence = { |baseFreq, dur = 0.5|

        [0, -0.8, 0.2, -0.3, 0],  // down, slight up, settle down
        [0.3, 0.1, 0.15, 0.1].normalizeSum * dur,

};
```

#### Sawari (Shamisen Buzz)

```supercollider
// Sawari - characteristic buzzing of shamisen
// The buzz occurs when string lightly touches the nut groove
~sawari = { |sig, amount = 0.3, freq = 120|
    var buzz = sig * (1 + (amount * SinOsc.ar(freq * [1, 2.3, 3.7]).sum.clip(-1, 1)));
    var rattle = sig * Decay.ar(Impulse.ar(freq * 0.5), 0.01) * amount * 0.5;
    sig + (buzz * amount) + rattle
};

// Full shamisen attack with sawari
~shamisenPluck = { |freq = 220, amp = 0.3|
    var pluck = Pluck.ar(
        PinkNoise.ar(0.5),
        Impulse.ar(0),
        freq.reciprocal,
        freq.reciprocal,
        3,
        0.5
    );
    var sawari = pluck * (1 + (0.2 * SinOsc.ar(freq * 2.03)));
    sawari * amp
};
```

#### Heterophony Generation

```supercollider
// Heterophony parameter generator (conceptual - returns variation data)
// Use these parameters to offset multiple Pbinds or synth voices
// Each voice has slight timing and ornament variations
~heterophony = { |melody, numVoices = 3, maxDelay = 0.15, ornamentProb = 0.3|
    numVoices.collect { |i|
        var delay = rrand(0, maxDelay);
        var pitchVar = rrand(-0.02, 0.02);  // slight pitch variation
        var ornament = if(ornamentProb.coin, {
            // Add grace note or kobushi
            rrand(-2, 2)  // semitone deviation
        }, { 0 });
        [delay, pitchVar, ornament]
    }
};

// Example: apply heterophony to a Pbind
// Each voice gets slightly different timing and pitch
~heteroVoice = { |basePattern, voiceNum, maxDelay = 0.1|
    Pbindf(basePattern,
        \lag, Pwhite(0, maxDelay),
        \detune, Pwhite(-5, 5),  // cents
        \amp, basePattern[\amp] * rrand(0.7, 1.0)
    )
};
```

#### Melodic Contours

```supercollider
// Descending phrase (common in folk songs)
~descendingPhrase = { |scale, startNote = 7|
    Array.series(rrand(4, 8), startNote, -1).collect { |i|
        scale.wrapAt(i)
    }
};

// Ornamental approach note
~approachNote = { |target, scale|
    var idx = scale.indexOf(target) ?? 0;
    [scale.wrapAt(idx + 1), target]  // step down to target
};
```
